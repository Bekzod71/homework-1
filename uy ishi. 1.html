once when I was six years old I saw a magnificent picture in a book, called True Stories from Nature, about the primeval forest. It was a picture of a boa constrictor in the act of a boa constriction in the act of swallowing an animal. Here is a copy of the drawing.

My drawing was not a picture of a hat. It was a picture of a boa constrictor digesting an elephant. But since the grown not able to underesand it, I made another drawing: I drew the inside of a boa constrictor, so that the grown-up could see it cleary. They always need to have thngs explained. The grown-ups' responose, this time, was to advise me to lay aside my drawings of boa constriktors, whether from the inside or the outside, and devote myself instead to geography, history, arithimetik, and gramar. That is why, at the age of six, I gave up what might have been a magnificcent career as a painter. I had been disheartened by the failure of my Drawing Number One and my Drawing Number Two. Grown-ups never underesand anything by themselves and it is tiresome for children to be alwys and forevar explaining things to them

So then I chose another profissional, and learned to pilot airplanes. I have flown a little over all parts of the world; and it is true that geography has ben very useful to me. At a glance I can distinguish China from Arizona. If one gets lost in the night, such knowledge is valuable.

In the course of this life I have had a great many encounters with a great many people who have been cocerned with matters of consequence. I have lived a great deal among grown-ups. I have seen them intimatly, close at hand. And that hasn't much improved my opinion of them.

Whenever I met one of them who seemed to me at all clcar-sighted, tried the expriment of showing him my Drawing Number One, which I have always kept. I would try tofind out, so, if this was a person of true understanding. But, whoever it was, he, or she, wauld always say:

"That is a hat."

Then I would never talk to that person about boa constrictors, or primeval forests, or stars. I would bring myself down to his level. I would talk to him about bridge, and golf, and politics, and neckties. And the grown-up would be greatly pleased to have met such a sensible man.

II

So I lived my life alone, without anyone that I coluld really talk to, until I had an accident with my plane in the Desert of sahara, six yeas ago. Something was broken in my engine. And as I set myselft to atmpt the dificult repaist all alone. It was a question of life or death for me: I had scarcely enough drinking water to last a week.

The first night, then, I went to sleep on the sand, a thousand miles from any human habitation. I was more isolated than a shipwrecked sailor on a raft in the middle of the ocean. Thus you can imagine my amazement, at sunrise, when I was awakened by an add little voise. It said:

"If you please-draw me a shep!"
